"What factors influence the shape of a West Nile Virus (WNV) outbreak, and How to prevent it?"


 Angelica Merced   May 2022

West Nile Virus (WNV) is an arbovirus discovered in Uganda's West Nile sub-region in 1927. The virus then spread to other countries, and in the mid-1990s, Algeria and Romania had their first severe WNV outbreak. The virus was first detected in the United States having its first case discovered in New York City in 1999. Within that year, many cases were reported, 62 human cases, 25 horse cases, and several bird diagnoses. Since then, WNV has been the most prevalent virus transmitted to humans by mosquitoes[1], with over 48,183 reports received by CDC or Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (from 1999 to 2017)[2].

On this account, many researchers conducted a study such as serosurveys that would examine how many humans and animals in a population have been infected with WNV and recovered with the virus; virus vector isolation and identification; experimental infection of birds and humans; and ecological assessments[3]. Findings from the studies significantly improved our understanding of the virus and how viruses spread.

A study entitled "West Nile Virus: Biology, Transmission, and Human Infection" found out that different mosquito species have varying abilities to acquire and transmit West Nile Virus (WNV), and Culex mosquitoes are widely considered the primary transmission vector[1]. The typical carriers of WNV to humans are mosquitoes previously eaten on an infected bird[4]. Thus, infected mosquito bites are the most common source of human infection. However, under normal circumstances, WNV does not spread by physical contact between humans

(person to person) or from animal to human (bird to human)[5]. However, there are few cases where transplantation of organs spreads the virus. Hence, according to health officials, the virus was likely obtained through a blood transfusion[6].

The moment the mosquito bite an infected bird, the virus will circulate in their blood for a few days. Afterward, the virus will enter the mosquito's salivary glands; thus, when bitten, the virus can proliferate in the body and cause sickness[7]. During the first severe outbreak of West Nile Virus in the Mediterranean basin, fever, headache, myalgias, anorexia, abdominal pain, exanthems, and vomiting were among the first clinical signs linked with the infection documented in detail[3].

Moreover, the interaction between mosquitoes and animal hosts help sustain WNV in nature; however, birds, particularly robins, serve as the most common and preferred reservoir. Because the dominant viral vectors prefer to feed on robins, the American robin is assumed to be the primary host species responsible for maintaining and transmitting WNV in the United States[1]. These robins are birds that are also called "super-spreaders" of WNV[8].

After reading up on the facts mentioned above, we already have some general idea of why humans and some animals get West Nile Virus. And that is because of getting bitten by an infected mosquito. However, to better understand how the transmission happened and know some factors that could affect the rate of the shape of WNV outbreak, we need to model and simulate the virus with the help of a multi-agent programmable modeling environment - Netlogo. However, let us first determine the simulation setup, such as its parameters and its limitations.

The following are parameters used in the simulation:

  • Number-of-Humans - the total number of susceptible humans.
  • Number-of-Mosquitoes - the total number of susceptible mosquitoes.
  • Number-of-Robins - the total number of susceptible robins.
  • Number-of-Cardinals - the total number of susceptible cardinals.
  • Infected-Robins - the total number of infected robins.
  • Host-Competence - the percentage of an infected robin to pass on the virus back again to mosquitoes when they get bitten.

The focus of this model is only on the cycle of infected robin - mosquito - susceptible robin, not the overall West Nile Virus effect. Moreover, it tries to simulate the summer season from June 1 to October 1 (particularly in the United States) - the days when mosquitoes are most active[9]. Also, when the simulation already start, no robins will be born, but some robins may die. Other animals can carry WNV, but for this model, only one were used which is the robin. Moreover, humans do not recover from WNV; they carry it forever.

Now let us introduce the population in the simulation. Green human icon represents a human or a person (susceptible), Yellow circle represents a mosquito (susceptible), Two-tone(black/red) bird icon represents a robin (susceptible) and lastly, blue bird icon represents a cardinal (susceptible).


When a susceptible mosquito tries to bite an infected robin - the bird with an orange color, the mosquito now has a chance to get infected. The red circle now represents the infected mosquito. Susceptible robins do get infected, which is the birds in the color black. Moreover, when the infected mosquito transmits the virus to a susceptible human, the human icon will change its color to red.

Let us do a simulation where there are around 500 mosquitoes in the environment or place, and out of 10 robins, there is only one infected robin with 20 people roaming around. Also, we try to apply only 0.2% host competence of the robins because according to CT.gov., there is a possibility where only one in every 500 mosquitos is infected[10]. Meaning the probability of mosquitoes getting infected with the virus is very low.

[Note: Click the Setup button to generate the population. Then, click Run.]

Notice that the WNV carrier mosquitoes infected no humans this is also because the chances of humans getting bitten by the infected mosquito is very low.

Let us try to increase the number of robins to 200 and the number of infected robins to 10; the rest will stay the same. What will probably happen?

Trying to run the model for at least 3 times, we noticed that mostly will result to having no human infected with the virus; this is somehow because of the host competence. The competence of the infected robins stays constant; thus, the probability of mosquitoes getting infected with the virus somehow remains the same.

Suppose we try to increase its host competence to at least 20% to see if there is a significant change.

We can see that more and more mosquitoes get infected as well as humans. Thus, a high-competence host can increase the likelihood of the virus spreading throughout the community[11].

In addition to the number of infected robins, other factors could minimize the spread of the West Nile Virus. One is the presence of a cardinal. According to Rebecca Levine, Ph.D., cardinals are like “super suppressors". Although cardinals are susceptible and can get infected with WNV, they are significantly less likely to have enough virus circulating in their blood to transmit the virus back to mosquitoes[12].

Now let us try to include 300 cardinals in the simulation, and the rest remains the same. Do cardinals really try to act as super suppressors?

We can see that having cardinals flying around somehow reduce the number of infected mosquitoes. This is also because the chances of mosquitoes to bite an infected robin will be lower because mosquitoes now have two choices of whom to bite.

Furthermore, adding another factor like some robins migrate - either infected or susceptible robins or both infected and susceptible - has the potential to lower the number of cases in that area. However, this could also be the reason why other places start to encounter West Nile Virus because there is a possibility that the birds that migrated to their places could be mostly infected.

The mosquito infection rate peaks in mid-July, around the same time as mosquitoes switch from feeding on robins to feeding on cardinals[13]. Recall that this simulation wants to simulate the summer season because it is said to be the high-risk season; so this is also when mosquitoes are most active[14]. So when we try to simulate again with a higher number of mosquitoes, this could be the result. More number of mosquitoes will get infected.


After doing the simulations, we now somehow understand how West Nile Virus spread to birds and humans. Moreover, to address how a West Nile Virus outbreak could recur, many factors must be considered as discussed above. If it is the summer season, mosquitoes will be in massive numbers, so there is a higher chance that a mosquito will bite an infected bird. Also, the number of super suppressor birds - cardinals in the place does have a significant effect. If the number of infected robins is greater than the number of cardinals, there is a higher chance that the virus will spread to other animals and humans.

Furthermore, we can obtain ideas regarding the prevention of West Nile Virus or reduction of the number of infections from the simulation itself. Since we saw how humans, mosquitoes, robins, and cardinals will interact with each other, we can also conclude that if there are no infected robins, no infections will happen in mosquitoes and humans, as well as no additional robins will get infected. However, that is not the case in reality that there will not be a single robin present in the environment. Thus, we need to look for other factors which we can manage, such as raising awareness and taking personal precautions to avoid mosquito bites, especially since there is still no licensed vaccine for humans[15]. Also, larval source reduction is the most efficient and cost-effective method of mosquito control.

Note that even if these simulations are just a representation of complex reality, these aid in predicting the likelihood and severity of an outbreak. Moreover, we can still somehow rely on them and make conclusions that would help people understand the virus in their respective places and improve their responses to viruses or diseases.
